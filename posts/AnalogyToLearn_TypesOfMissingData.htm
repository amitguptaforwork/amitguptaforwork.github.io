📘 MCAR, MAR, MNAR — The Classroom Story

1. MCAR – Missing Completely At Random

Story: A sudden gust of wind 🌬️ blows some kids’ homework papers out the window. It had nothing to do with how good or bad their work was or who they are. Pure chance!

Key idea: Missingness has nothing to do with the student or the score. Dropping the missing ones still gives you a representative sample.

💡 Real-world analogy: A lab machine randomly crashes and corrupts some test results.

2. MAR – Missing At Random

Story: Students who play football ⚽ often forget to hand in homework because practice runs late. But within the football group, the decision to hand in has nothing to do with how good their homework was.

Key idea: Missingness is related to some observed factor (football), but not the actual score.

💡 Real-world analogy: Older survey respondents are less likely to report income → depends on age (observed), not income value itself.

3. MNAR – Missing Not At Random

Story: Some students hide their homework if it’s really bad 🫣. The worse the homework, the more likely it goes missing.

Key idea: Missingness depends directly on the value itself (bad scores → more missing). Hardest case to deal with.

💡 Real-world analogy: High-income people avoid disclosing income → missingness depends on the true income itself.

📝 Recap

  • MCAR: Wind blows the papers away → purely random.
  • MAR: Football kids forget → depends on observed (football), not score.
  • MNAR: Kids hide bad homework → depends on actual missing value itself.
